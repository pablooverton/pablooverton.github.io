I went back to Neal Stephenson's *Anathem* this year, and a book I had filed under "big speculative novel about math monks" turned out to be the most accurate thing I have read about 2026.

Here is the setup. On the world of the book, the thinkers live behind walls. Mathematicians, philosophers, and scientists, called the avout, are sealed inside cloisters called concents and cut off from the consumer society outside. The gates open on a schedule: once a year for some orders, once a decade, a century, or a thousand years for others. They call the opening the Apert. Inside, almost no personal technology. Outside, a loud commercial culture that has already collapsed and rebuilt itself several times. The avout keep the deep knowledge alive precisely by refusing to be plugged into all of it.

That reads like a metaphor for a monastery. Coming back to it, I think it is closer to a warning about the internet.

## Artificial inanity

The detail that stopped me is almost thrown away. The network the outside world runs on, the Reticulum, is so polluted that no one trusts it, and the pollution is deliberate. Systems churn out enormous volumes of plausible, useless content and scatter it everywhere, so that any real signal drowns under fabricated noise. If you want to hide something, you do not delete it. You bury it under ten thousand convincing fakes.

Stephenson published that in 2008. The book even names the practice, and the name is a pun that has aged into a warning: artificial inanity. The dumb twin of artificial intelligence.

We have a word for the result now. We call it AI slop: the auto-generated articles, the SEO pages written by nobody for nobody, the reviews of products that do not exist. The mechanism arrived almost exactly as described, down to the joke. The thing worth fearing was never a superintelligence. It was stupidity produced at infinite scale and near-zero cost, and it is already here.

I build with these models for a living, and I am not a doomer about them. But the failure mode Stephenson named is the real one, and most of the public argument about AI is pointed at the wrong target.

## The Ita

A second detail lands differently once you write software.

Someone inside the walls still has to run the machines. The avout do theory. The networks and computers are handled by a separate group, the Ita, who live inside the concent but apart from the scholars. They are essential and they are looked down on. The thinkers depend on them completely and treat them like plumbing.

If you have ever been the engineer in a room full of people who want the output of a system while treating the system itself as beneath them, you have been Ita. Stephenson clearly respects the Ita more than his own characters do, and the point is quiet but sharp: the people who keep the infrastructure alive are not a lesser version of the people who use it. They are the reason any of the thinking can happen at all.

## The case for the wall

The avout's real technology is not a machine. It is the wall, and the schedule for when it opens.

By controlling their own connectivity, they buy the one thing the outside world has lost: long, uninterrupted attention. The thousand-year orders work on problems across spans no modern institution could hold together. Most of us cannot hold a single thought for a thousand seconds before something buzzes. The book takes the ordinary complaint about distraction and pushes it until it becomes a design decision at the scale of a civilization: how connected should the people doing the hardest work actually be?

I do not think the answer is a literal monastery. But my own best work happens under small private versions of the Apert, the gate shut for a few hours, the network off, the slop locked outside. *Anathem* gave me a cleaner way to say why that works. It is not willpower. It is architecture. You build the wall, and the wall does the work.

## Why it holds up

The famous parts of the book are the big questions: whether mathematical truth is discovered or invented, whether minds on different worlds reach for the same forms, what consciousness is even made of. Those alone are worth the price of entry. What I did not expect on a reread was how practical the thing turned out to be. It predicted the texture of our information world, named the real danger with a pun, and gave dignity to the people who keep the machines running.

For a novel about cloistered math monks, it has aged like a documentary.
